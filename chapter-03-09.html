CHAPTER RESEARCH EDITION

The argument, evidence and visual route

This chapter is expanded through the research plan below. It is written as a reading route, not a placeholder checklist.

  • Narrative angle: access to books beyond the textbook as a proxy for a richer, more unequal "extra-curricular" learning environment.
  • Historical context: public library system's uneven state-level development (library legislation exists in only some states); the National Book Trust's mandate vs. reach.
  • Mechanism: home literacy environment (books in the house, read-aloud practice) as a predictor of reading comprehension independent of school quality — a well-established general finding in education research, stated generically rather than with an invented India-specific number unless verified.
  • India context: ASER's long-running annual assessment is the standout India-specific data source for reading-level outcomes and should anchor this chapter.
  • Counterarguments: digital access (even basic smartphone-based reading apps) is partially substituting for physical books in some populations — worth noting as a genuine, complicating trend, connecting forward to Part IX.
  • Limitations: library-access and home-book-ownership data are patchy at the national level.
  • Data [VERIFY]: ASER reading-level statistics (proportion of children at grade-level reading); any available library-density or membership data.
  • Chart: ASER reading-level trend over available years.
  • Map: none essential unless state library-density data is sourced.
  • Visual: a community/public library reading scene.
  • Sources: ASER annual reports (Pratham), state public library legislation records.
