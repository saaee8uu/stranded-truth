Opening. A child's fifth birthday is treated, almost everywhere, as an unremarkable milestone. In practice it is closer to a checkpoint: by then, most of the biological and cognitive architecture a person will build the rest of their life on top of is already substantially laid down. The five years before school is where nutrition, illness, stimulation and safety do their most irreversible work — and where India's inequalities are least visible, because the children affected are too young to be counted in school enrollment figures, labour statistics, or almost any of the data systems the rest of this book relies on. This chapter is about the years the country's data collects least and needs most.

History. India's institutional answer to this gap is the Integrated Child Development Services (ICDS) scheme, launched in 1975 and still, on paper, one of the largest early-childhood programmes in the world by number of centres. Its core unit is the anganwadi — a village- or neighbourhood-level centre meant to combine supplementary nutrition, health referral, and pre-school stimulation in one place, staffed by a single worker (with an assistant, where sanctioned) for a catchment that can run into hundreds of children and mothers. Successive governments have re-branded and re-funded the scheme (most recently as "Saksham Anganwadi and Poshan 2.0"), which is worth naming plainly: this is not a new idea being tried for the first time, it is a fifty-year-old idea still being re-engineered because the first several attempts did not close the gap it was built for.

Scale. The National Family Health Survey (NFHS-5, 2019–21) is the standard source for how large that gap still is: national and state-level figures exist for stunting (low height-for-age, reflecting chronic undernutrition), wasting (low weight-for-height, reflecting acute undernutrition) and underweight prevalence among children under five [VERIFY: exact NFHS-5 national and state figures before publication — these numbers are already used correctly elsewhere in this build's Data Room and should be pulled from there for consistency rather than re-sourced]. What matters for this chapter is less the single national number than the range across states, which NFHS-5 also reports and which is often wider than the change over an entire survey round.

Geography. Anganwadi infrastructure quality — a pucca (permanent, weatherproof) building with a toilet and drinking water versus a rented room or open space — varies sharply by state and is tracked, imperfectly, through Ministry of Women and Child Development monitoring data [VERIFY: current MWCD infrastructure-compliance figures]. This is one of the clearer cases in the whole book where the administrative capacity of a state government, not the wealth of individual households, is doing a large share of the explanatory work.

Mechanism. The economic case for early-childhood investment — that a rupee spent closing a nutrition or stimulation gap before age five returns more than the same rupee spent correcting its downstream effects at age fifteen — is one of the more robust findings in development economics generally, and it is the reason ICDS exists at all. The mechanism is biological and cognitive: chronic undernutrition in the first 1,000 days affects brain development in ways that are difficult or impossible to fully reverse later; a stimulating, safe early environment builds the cognitive and emotional scaffolding that formal schooling then builds on rather than starts from scratch.

Counter-evidence. It would be wrong to read this as a story of uniform failure. Stunting and underweight prevalence have fallen across successive NFHS rounds in most states, and some states with strong ICDS implementation and complementary sanitation/health investment show outcomes closer to middle-income-country averages than to the poorest-performing Indian states. Coverage — a child actually being registered at and attending an anganwadi — has also expanded significantly since the 1990s. The honest version of this chapter's claim is not "the system doesn't work," but "the system's success is currently uneven enough that a child's outcome still depends heavily on which state, and which specific anganwadi, they were born near."

Lived reality. The gap between what ICDS is designed to deliver and what an individual anganwadi can actually deliver often comes down to one person: the anganwadi worker, an "honorary" (not formally salaried, technically) frontline worker who is simultaneously a nutrition-supplement distributor, a pre-school teacher, a health-referral point, and — increasingly — a data-entry point for multiple digital tracking systems, for an honorarium far below a living wage in most states [VERIFY: current state-wise honorarium ranges]. Her caseload, training, and working conditions are arguably a better predictor of a given centre's real quality than the state's aggregate ICDS budget.

Connections. This chapter is the direct foundation for Chapter 03-02 ("Anganwadi India," which should focus on the worker herself in more depth), Chapter 03-03 ("Hunger and Hidden Hunger," which separates calorie sufficiency from micronutrient adequacy), and reaches back to Part I Chapter 07 ("The Body at the Starting Line"), which introduces the same NFHS-5 nutrition indicators from the birth-moment angle this chapter picks up from age one onward.

Verdict. STRONGLY SUPPORTED, WITH LARGE AND MEASURABLE STATE VARIATION. The claim that early-childhood conditions matter disproportionately, and that India's institutional response (ICDS/anganwadi) has real but geographically uneven reach, is well supported by NFHS-5 cross-state data and by the general early-childhood-development literature. It is not supported to claim national failure or national success as a single verdict — the state-level variance is the finding.
