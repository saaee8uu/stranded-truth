FULL RESEARCH DRAFT · INTEGRATED

Opening. Ask most people to picture "child labour" in India and they will likely picture a factory floor. The far more common reality, and the harder one to legislate against, is a child helping in a family shop after school, working a family's agricultural land during a harvest, or doing paid domestic work in someone else's home. Indian law now treats these three situations very differently from each other — and that difference is itself the story this chapter needs to tell.

History. The Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Act, first passed in 1986, banned employment of children below 14 in a list of hazardous occupations and processes. A significant amendment in 2016 tightened the age-14 ban to cover all occupations (not just a hazardous list) — but simultaneously carved out an exception permitting children to help in family enterprises or as artists outside school hours, subject to conditions. That single amendment is worth a full paragraph in this chapter on its own: it was defended as recognising the reality of India's informal, family-run economy, where a blanket ban was seen as both unenforceable and, for some poor households, damaging to livelihood. It was criticised, including by child-rights organisations, as opening a loophole large enough to legalise a great deal of what most people would still call child labour, since "family enterprise" is broad and difficult to inspect.

Scale. The Census of India's work-participation data for the 5–14 age group is the standard national-level measure, and it showed a substantial decline in the child-work-participation rate between the 2001 and 2011 Census rounds [VERIFY: exact 2001 and 2011 figures]. There is no equivalent 2021 figure, because the 2021 Census was postponed; this chapter should say so explicitly rather than let the 2011 figure quietly stand in as current.

Geography. Child labour prevalence by the Census measure varies by state and is concentrated, though not exclusively, in agriculture-dependent and lower-income states; it also has a strong rural skew, connecting to Part I Chapter 09's rural/urban framing.

Mechanism. The core economic logic is a household-level trade-off: where the marginal value of a child's labour or income (in cash, or in freeing an adult to do paid work) is high relative to the marginal value of the schooling the child would otherwise be getting — because the local school is poor quality, far away, or both — households are more likely to choose work over school, especially during agricultural peak seasons or economic shocks. This is not a claim that poor families don't value education; it is a claim about constrained choice under real trade-offs, and the chapter should be careful to frame it that way.

Counter-evidence. The Right to Education Act (2009) and the broader expansion of school access and mid-day meal programmes have coincided with rising enrollment and, per the Census trend cited above, falling reported child work-participation. Whether school-access expansion caused the decline, or both moved together for other reasons (rising household incomes, for instance), is a genuine empirical question this chapter should not overclaim an answer to.

Lived reality. Family-enterprise and domestic work are structurally harder to observe than factory work, which means official statistics almost certainly undercount them. This chapter should be explicit that the "true" prevalence of child labour, especially in its 2016-amendment-permitted forms, is not something the Census or any single Indian dataset currently measures well.

Connections. Links forward to Part IV (school retention and dropout, especially Chapter 04-10 on the credential-to-job pipeline) and to Part V (informal labour markets, since child workers typically enter the same informal economy adults do).

Verdict. STRONGLY SUPPORTED THAT SCALE HAS FALLEN; DISPUTED/UNRESOLVED ON HOW MUCH THE 2016 AMENDMENT'S FAMILY-ENTERPRISE EXCEPTION UNDERCOUNTS REMAINING CHILD LABOUR. This dual verdict — real progress on the measured trend, real measurement uncertainty on the exempted category — should be stated as the chapter's actual conclusion rather than resolved into a single simpler claim.

Source trail. Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Act 1986 and 2016 Amendment (full text); Census of India work-participation tables, 2001 and 2011; ILO and UNICEF India child-labour estimates (integrator to pull latest published figures with methodology notes); RTE Act 2009.
