Chapter 01

Why the Education System Was Created

The education system was created to teach a single language or a fixed group of subjects through which a certain level of intelligence (IQ) could be developed. However, this development often provides little real benefit to life itself. If we look at the other side of the coin, the system was also designed to enforce the rule of the instructor—meaning obedience to authority.

Going Back to the Beginning

From the moment we are born, we are shaped by society. We speak according to society and live according to society. Very early in life, the belief that education is essential is planted in us. The goal becomes being something or achieving something that others have already achieved.

Society teaches us that only the “first” is remembered, and this idea pushes individuals to follow already-created paths rather than discovering new ones. Education is presented as the key to respect, money, and the fulfillment of dreams. We are told that if we follow this system, we will eventually achieve everything we desire. But the real question remains—do we actually achieve it?

The Child and the System

A child is naturally a true explorer of reality. Curiosity, creativity, and questioning are part of a child’s nature. However, under the influence of society, elders or guardians place the child into the education system. Inside this system, the child’s thoughts are confined into a structured framework, where tasks are assigned, ordered, and repeated. Instead of exploring freely, the child learns to function in shifts, following instructions rather than developing independent thought.

Observing One’s Life

We are born free, carrying countless thoughts and possibilities. Over time, we are told that the education system is the answer to life. Believing this, we enter the system and slowly begin to assume that we are learning everything that exists and everything that matters. In reality, many of our ancestors lived without formal education systems, yet they understood life deeply. Despite this, we fall into a trap where our thinking is reshaped. We become imprisoned within the deceptive structure of education, where creativity and genuine understanding are gradually replaced by illusion and false certainty. What Is Education?

Education is a concept created to study and understand the outcomes of previous knowledge and discoveries. It exists to help us understand fundamental laws, principles, and the foundations of language and human understanding. Education should be studied not for status or validation, but for understanding what has existed before, what exists now, and what may exist in the future.

In its original sense, education was not limited to academics alone. It included multiple branches:

  • Knowledge
  • Human Affairs
  • Consistency
  • Human Understanding
  • Manners
  • Coordination of Human Welfare
  • Piety

The Present Condition of the Education System

In practice, education has become a tool for serving society rather than individuals. Today, education is pursued mainly to obtain certificates and degrees—paper education.

In the modern age, value is equated with paper qualifications. These papers themselves have no intrinsic value, yet they artificially grant respect, happiness, social status, manners, and even moral credibility. This image has been carefully constructed and promoted through societal propaganda.

Is Education Really Built for Us?

The education system was designed in a way that limits questioning of reality. By placing individuals in competitive races, strict timelines, and pressure-based challenges, it discourages deep reflection and independent thought.

Education becomes a method of linguistic knowledge transfer that appears to enlighten, while indirectly restricting the ability to question fundamentals. This system heavily relies on competition. According to human psychology, people are driven to complete assigned tasks within limited timeframes and to outperform others, even if the task itself lacks meaning. Historical Roots of the System - Prussia and the Birth of Modern Education

I began tracing the roots of the modern education system and reached Prussia, a small European state that changed the world quietly.

Before the nineteenth century, Prussia did not believe in universal education. Learning was limited to elites, clergy, and specialists. Ordinary people learned through life, work, family, and survival. There was no fixed system, no standard curriculum, and no mass schooling. Everything changed in 1806. That year, Prussia suffered a crushing defeat at the hands of Napoleon Bonaparte at the Battle of Jena–Auerstedt. The defeat was shocking. Prussia had soldiers, weapons, and discipline, yet it collapsed in front of Napoleon’s army.

Prussian elites were disturbed. The loss was not just military; it was a failure of structure. A Prussian officer later admitted: “We were not defeated by strength, but by organization.”

The ruling class began asking difficult questions. Why did Napoleon’s army succeed? The answer they reached was simple and dangerous: Napoleon’s soldiers obeyed without hesitation. Orders were followed instantly. There was no room for personal interpretation. Prussian leaders concluded that individual thinking was a weakness when it came to power.

Between 1807 and 1810, Prussian reformers, generals, and philosophers gathered to redesign the state. They believed that if citizens were trained early, obedience would become natural.

One belief guided them: “The state must shape the citizen before the citizen can shape the state.”

In 1810, under the leadership of Wilhelm von Humboldt, Prussia introduced the first compulsory, state-controlled education system.

The purpose was clear. Schools were designed to look and function like systems:

Children grouped by age Fixed subjects Standard textbooks Bells to control time Exams to measure obedience Punishment for resistance Rewards for conformity Humboldt openly stated: “Education must form disciplined citizens for the service of the state.”

Obedience, Control, and the Rise of the System

Even though they were fewer in number, they rebelled and won the war against Prussia. This puzzled the Prussians. If Prussia had a massive army, then why did they lose the war? What was the real cause?

This question led to clarity: the loss was due to a lack of command and unity. Orders were not properly followed. Soldiers were acting independently, executing their own plans. Creativity existed, but unity and obedience did not exist as a collective force among them.

At a certain point, they understood something crucial: one cannot completely destroy creativity and imagination, but one can set boundaries and limitations. By applying structured pressure, restless thoughts, questions, creativity, and imagination can be redirected, controlled, and subconsciously aligned to serve reality as defined by authority.

This system was not created to produce free thinkers. It was created to produce reliable citizens. Military thinking entered the classroom. A famous Prussian military principle became cultural truth: “A good soldier does not ask why. He obeys.” Schools adopted the same logic.

By 1815, after Napoleon’s defeat, Prussia had transformed. The army was stronger. The economy stabilized. Citizens followed rules. Rebellion reduced. The experiment worked. Other nations noticed. During the Industrial Revolution, England adopted the Prussian model to train factory workers. Discipline and punctuality were more valuable than creativity. France adopted it to create loyalty after repeated revolutions. In the 1840s, an American reformer, Horace Mann, studied Prussian schools and brought the system to the United States. He admired its order and efficiency. He wrote: “Education must train the people to be orderly and productive.” By the late nineteenth century, the Prussian model had spread across Europe and North America. Colonial Expansion and the Indian Context - British education system

As British trade emerged as one of the strongest centers of global exchange, led by the East India Company, many Indian merchants were already well-established. Trade flourished, and Indians benefited economically through exchange and commerce. This was not a problem for Indian merchants—but it was a problem for the British. The British faced a linguistic barrier. Indians did not know English, and many were illiterate by British standards. This made the British dependent on local systems, reducing their control and profit.

They realized that by implanting an education system, they could solve multiple problems: Create cheap labor

In 1835, the British applied the same structure in India. Thomas Babington Macaulay, speaking honestly, wrote: “We must create a class of persons Indian in blood and color, but English in taste, opinions, morals, and intellect.”

So,they exactly worked upon it as it was reliable stock for their approach of colonization and took down measures and had thoughts that were:

Produce obedient workers Increase consumption Strengthen colonial control Reduce resistance However, this move also triggered resistance. Indians rebelled against British authority as colonization intensified yet we follow up till date the same curriculum

How the British Education System Emerged in India

When the British first came to India, education was not their goal. Their purpose was trade, profit, and control.

In the early 17th century, the East India Company entered India as a trading body. India already had strong systems of learning—madrasas, gurukuls, maktabs, and traditional apprenticeships. Education existed, but it was local, cultural, and independent. For a long time, the British did not interfere with these systems.

The Real Problem the British Faced As British control expanded in the 18th century, they faced serious problems:

Language barriers Lack of local administrators Dependence on Indian scholars and translators Difficulty controlling law, revenue, and governance British officers did not understand Indian languages, customs, or legal systems. This made them dependent on locals, which weakened their authority.

They needed Indians who would: Speak English Understand British law Follow British orders Think like the British This is where education entered—not as enlightenment, but as strategy.

Early Attempts (Late 1700s – Early 1800s)

Initially, the British supported Oriental education—teaching Persian, Arabic, and Sanskrit. Institutions like the Calcutta Madrasa (1781) and Sanskrit College (1791) were created to train Indians for administration using traditional knowledge. But this approach failed to give the British full control. Indian scholars still thought independently. Cultural loyalty remained strong. The British wanted something deeper—mental alignment.

The Turning Point: 1835

The real shift happened in 1835, with the intervention of Thomas Babington Macaulay. In his famous Minute on Education, Macaulay openly stated the British plan:

“We must at present do our best to form a class who may be interpreters between us and the millions whom we govern—a class of persons Indian in blood and color, but English in taste, opinions, morals, and intellect.”

This was not hidden. It was direct. Macaulay rejected traditional Indian education and argued that English education was superior and more useful for British administration. What Changed After Macaulay

After 1835:

English became the medium of instruction Western subjects replaced traditional knowledge Education focused on clerical and administrative skills Moral and cultural systems were subtly reshaped Education was now designed to create: Clerks Accountants Teachers Lawyers Low-level administrators These roles supported British rule without giving real power.

Universities and Expansion (1854–1857)

In 1854, the Wood’s Despatch formally structured education in India. It recommended: Universities Teacher training Standardized curriculum Examination system.

Soon after, in 1857, universities were established in: Calcutta Bombay Madras These universities did not encourage innovation or questioning. Their main function was certification. A British official view at the time was clear: Educated Indians should assist governance, not challenge it. Education After the 1857 Revolt.The Indian Rebellion of 1857 deeply frightened the British. After this:

Education became more controlled Loyalty was prioritized over intelligence Critical thinking was discouraged Obedience became central Schools were shaped to reduce rebellion, not inspire freedom. Education became a tool to produce stability, not awareness.

The Nature of British Education in India

British education in India was: Book-based, not life-based Exam-oriented, not understanding-oriented Job-focused, not purpose-focused It rewarded: Memorization Compliance English fluency And punished: Questioning Cultural independence Intellectual rebellion Many Indians began to believe that British education meant superiority, and traditional knowledge meant backwardness.

This belief went deep. Long-Term Impact

By the late 19th and early 20th century:

  • English education became the gatekeeper of success
  • Government jobs required degrees
  • Social status depended on certificates
  • Even after independence, the structure remained.
  • The rulers changed.
  • The system did not.

Education became a tool of control, not liberation. Many people today believe this system began in 1990. That is not true. What happened in the 1990s was not creation, but upgradation. Computers entered classrooms. Degrees multiplied. Competition increased. But the structure remained the same. The system born in 1810 simply went digital.

By the end of the twentieth century, kings and colonizers were gone, but the education system remained. Corporations replaced empires. Employees replaced soldiers. Deadlines replaced commands.

The foundation never changed. One quiet truth remains: “An obedient mind is easier to manage than a free one.” Prussia lost a war in 1806. The world inherited its solution - a proper system labelled as an education system.

Present Scenario :

Hundreds of years have passed, yet the conditioning remains. Once we served kings with our talents. Then we served the British. Now we serve corporations, institutions, and systems that condition us to remain mediocre and restrained. We are trained to strive only within predefined limits. We are conditioned by society from birth. On one side, failure is feared. On the other hand, success is defined narrowly through education. No one speaks about the other side of life—death, meaning, or freedom. There is no time to reflect, only pressure to perform. Education is made the major subject of life. Success becomes self-centered and goal-oriented, defined only through academic or professional achievement. This creates a repetitive spell—millions walk the same path, chasing success already achieved by others. The question remains: who will free us from this path?

Is It a Success Pie, Rat Race, or Matrix?

We are taught that the bloom of life comes only through education. We are conditioned to believe that unless we achieve something through this system, we are nothing. Slowly, we fall under a spell where we are trained to work and obey. Our exceptional abilities vanish through continuous acceptance of the idea that we are useless without validation. The motive became clear: unless you achieve what the system defines as success, you are considered worthless. If you follow the given matrix, you may bloom—but only as an exception. You will be rewarded according to how useful you are to the system. Society enables the belief that a person is nothing unless they work according to the matrix. We acquire concepts and definitions of success as the main subject of life. Success, in reality, is crowned to many—but not as kings, rather as successful, educated, obedient workers. Hence, knowledge is acquired not to liberate but to plant pessimism and realism that limit creativity. Free will is restricted. Freedom itself is presented as something achieved only through obedience. This pattern is visible everywhere—in jobs, businesses, teaching, and every occupation. We are taught to chase superiority instead of simply living by free will.
