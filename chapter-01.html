You are born before you can consent to anything.

OPENING SCENE

Before the first form is signed, before the first exam is failed, before the first salary is earned, a map has already begun to matter. The nearest hospital has a distance. The school has a language. The family has a balance sheet. The street has a postal code. The child has none of these choices, but all of them have already entered the story.

You do not choose the state, district, street, hospital, family, surname, language, religion, caste location, wealth, debt, nutrition, disability, gender expectations or social network waiting outside the room. Yet these facts can influence the distance between you and almost everything that comes later: a safe childhood, a good school, a doctor, clean air, a document, a job interview, a loan, a courtroom, a house, a voice that is heard.

This book begins there—not because birth explains destiny, and not because every Indian life can be reduced to a list of disadvantages. India contains mobility, resistance, generosity, invention and lives that escape every prediction. But freedom is not the same thing as an equal starting line.

The geography of birth

“India” is too large a unit to describe a childhood. A national average can be accurate and still fail to describe the district where a child is born. The public systems encountered in infancy are administered through states, districts, municipalities, villages and facilities whose capacity is uneven.

Geography works through ordinary mechanisms. Distance changes whether a clinic can be reached. Transport changes the price of that distance. Language changes whether a school feels intelligible. Local labour markets change what older children see adults doing for a living. Electricity, water, internet access and public administration alter the time required to do almost everything else.

The first inheritance is not property

It is position.

Some children inherit land. Some inherit rent. Some inherit debt. Some inherit a family name that opens a door; others inherit a name that causes a landlord, employer or neighbour to ask another question. Some are born close to hospitals, schools and transport. Others spend time and money merely reaching what policy documents call “access.”

Wealth matters not only because it purchases things. It purchases buffers. A failed exam can be repeated. A period without work can be survived. A medical bill can be paid without selling an asset. A move can be financed. Time can be bought for study rather than work. The same setback can therefore have radically different consequences.

The first lie of a broken system is that everybody entered it at the same door.
WHAT THE EVIDENCE SUPPORTS

Opportunity is inherited imperfectly—but measurably.

Asher, Novosad and Rafkin’s peer-reviewed study finds intergenerational mobility in India remained low and broadly constant across the period they study. The pattern is not uniform: mobility rose among Scheduled Caste sons while declining among Muslim sons.

WHAT IT DOES NOT PROVE

No group average predicts a person.

Mobility research describes distributions and changing odds. It does not assign destiny, ability or worth to an individual, and it does not make geography, caste, religion or family background a complete explanation.

One life, many systems

Imagine one child. Do not give the child a name yet. A name would tempt us to decide too quickly who the child represents.

The child needs nutrition, so the story enters food systems, household income, maternal health and public welfare. The child needs proof of existence, so the story enters documentation and bureaucracy. The child reaches school, so language, geography, teacher capacity, examinations and family wealth enter the room. The child seeks work, and the story moves into degrees, networks, informality, migration, automation and hierarchy.

Nothing stays in its own chapter. A school problem can become a labour problem. A labour problem can become a debt problem. Debt can become a health problem. A water problem can become a migration problem. Migration can become a housing problem. Housing can become a policing problem.

The distance between a right and a service

A right can exist in law while remaining expensive in practice. The cost may be a bus fare, a day of lost wages, a form that requires another form, an office that opens during working hours, a language barrier, a queue, a bribe, a social permission or the simple uncertainty of not knowing what happens next.

This is why the project will distinguish availability from access. A hospital on a district list is not the same as timely care. A school building is not the same as learning. Internet coverage is not the same as a usable device, data allowance, electricity and a quiet place to work.

Progress and failure can exist at the same time

A serious book about India cannot survive on outrage alone. National indicators can improve while particular states, districts or groups remain far behind. A public programme can save lives and still exclude people. A market can create opportunity and concentration at once. A family can protect and control the same child.

The method therefore refuses two easy stories: that nothing works, and that progress cancels inequality. Both stories are too simple for the evidence.

The method of this book

  1. State the claim narrowly. Avoid slogans that cannot be tested.
  2. Find primary evidence first. Official data, law, budgets, court records and first-hand records establish scale.
  3. Look for contradiction. Evidence that weakens the argument is part of the argument.
  4. Explain mechanism. A pattern is not an explanation.
  5. Connect systems. Health, money, law, family, geography and technology rarely remain separate.
  6. Publish a provisional verdict. Proven, strongly supported, disputed, plausible or unsupported—and open to revision.
