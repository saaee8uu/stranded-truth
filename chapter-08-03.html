FULL RESEARCH DRAFT · INTEGRATED

This chapter has the best existing data foundation of any Tier-C chapter — `groundwater.svg` and a Central Ground Water Board 2024 reference already exist in the build (`VISUAL-SOURCES.md`, `research-data.json`). Draft below is written to be the anchor point that asset should attach to, rather than rotate onto unrelated chapters.

Opening. Water scarcity in India is rarely a story about total national supply — it is a story about extraction outpacing recharge in specific aquifers, about who gets piped supply and who depends on tankers or private borewells, and about a resource that is legally almost unregulated at the point where most of it is actually taken: the individual landowner's well.

History. Groundwater in India has historically been treated, in law, as attached to land ownership — meaning a landowner has broad rights to extract groundwater beneath their own property with limited state oversight, a legal structure that predates the scale of extraction the Green Revolution's tube-well irrigation later enabled from the 1960s onward. That mismatch — 19th/20th-century property law meeting late-20th-century extraction technology — is a specific, citable, and underexplained structural cause worth naming directly.

Scale. The Central Ground Water Board's periodic "Dynamic Ground Water Resources of India" assessment (most recently 2024, already referenced elsewhere in this build) is the standard national source, and it categorises assessment units (blocks/mandals/talukas) by extraction stage — safe, semi-critical, critical, over-exploited [VERIFY: current national count/share of assessment units in each category from the 2024 report].

Geography. Over-exploitation is heavily concentrated in specific regions — parts of the northwestern agricultural belt in particular, driven by water-intensive crop patterns supported by both cropping incentives and free or subsidised electricity for pumping — while other regions remain comparatively "safe" by the same assessment. This is precisely the kind of finding a real choropleth map (recommended in `MISSING_VISUALS_DATA_MAPS.md`) would communicate far better than prose.

Mechanism. Three policy levers interact: crop-pricing incentives (Minimum Support Price structures historically favouring water-intensive crops like paddy in some states), input subsidies (free or flat-rate agricultural electricity removing the marginal cost of pumping), and weak extraction regulation (the land-ownership-based legal default described above). Addressing any one lever alone, without the other two, has had limited effect in past state-level attempts — a genuinely important nuance for the "mechanism" section.

Counter-evidence. Some states and specific watershed-management programmes have demonstrated real aquifer recovery through participatory groundwater management, crop diversification incentives, and metered/rationalised electricity pricing — worth citing as proof of concept rather than treating over-extraction as an unsolvable trend.

Lived reality. For households without reliable piped supply, the daily "water day" — the time spent walking to a source, queuing at a tanker, or paying a private borewell operator — is itself an unmeasured but real time-and-money cost, disproportionately borne by women and girls, connecting this chapter back to Part II Chapter 06 (domestic labour) and Part III Chapter 10 (unequal ownership of time).

Connections. Direct Atlas link to System 10 (Villages, Water and Resources — "Groundwater depletion," "Tanker economies," "Private borewells," all currently unresearched) and System 07 ("Groundwater" appears there too, under Food and Agriculture — a useful cross-system link showing how the same resource shows up in both the urban/domestic and agricultural framing).

Verdict. PROVEN NATIONAL PATTERN, UNEVENLY DISTRIBUTED, WITH IDENTIFIABLE POLICY LEVERS. This is one of the more solidly evidenced chapters this book can currently write, given the existing CGWB data source.

Source trail. Central Ground Water Board, Dynamic Ground Water Resources of India (2024); state groundwater/electricity-subsidy policy documents (integrator to name specific states' schemes); participatory groundwater management case studies (integrator to select specific, citable programme evaluations).
